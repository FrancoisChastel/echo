On slowness

a small theory of counterweights, written in the spring of mmxxvi

There is a particular pleasure in doing one thing slowly when the world is fast — not as a protest, exactly, but as a counterweight. The pleasure isn't the slowness itself. It's the noticing it permits.

When I watch a film at 1.5× speed, I can tell you what happens. I can't tell you how it felt, because feeling needs a tempo. The actors' breath, the silence after a line, the small adjustment of a hand on a table — these are the places a film actually lives. Take them away and you have a synopsis, not a story.

The same is true of the work I find I want to make. I can produce more if I produce faster, and what I produce will be technically correct, and it will not be what I meant. What I meant happens at the speed of attention.

an aside on tools

This is not a manifesto against speed. Speed is useful for things that don't need to be felt — sending an email, ordering groceries, writing a function whose behavior you already understand. Slowness is the technique you reach for when the texture of the result matters.

Tools are not neutral here. A tool that makes you faster at a thing that wants slowness is a tool that quietly changes what you make. You will not notice the change in any single instance — only later, looking back across a year of work, and realizing the work has lost some quality you cannot name.

the shortest distance between two points is a straight line. the most interesting one rarely is.

I am not advocating for inefficiency. I am advocating for the specific deliberate slowness that comes from caring how something is made, not just whether it is finished. The two are different and they produce different things.

If you read this far slowly, the essay was for you. If you skimmed, it was also for you — but for a different reason, and the next thing you do today might be a place to try the other way.

— françois
